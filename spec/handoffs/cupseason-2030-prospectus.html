CUPSEASON · FOUNDING PROSPECTUSSERIES A · DESIGNED FOR 2030

Where amateur golf counts.

An amateur plays twelve hundred rounds in a lifetime and keeps almost none of them. The score fades, the bet is forgotten, the trash talk scrolls away, the Saturdays blur. Golf is the most measured amateur sport on earth and somehow the least remembered. We are going to fix that — and in fixing it, build the operating system for an amateur golfer's competitive life.

PART 0You asked us to tell you when you're solving the wrong problem

Three corrections before a single screen gets designed

1 · Fantasy football is the wrong skeleton.

Fantasy sports exist because fans can't play — the draft, the squad, the points engine are all machinery for simulating participation in someone else's game. Golfers can play. The product should not simulate competition; it should orchestrate real competition. Season-long cups stay — they're the heartbeat — but drafts, squads, and fantasy scaffolding are optional formats, never the spine. The spine is simpler and older: a group of friends, a stretch of time, something at stake.

2 · The scorecard is not the loop. The gathering is.

The highest-frequency behavior in amateur golf isn't scoring — it's organizing. The group chat fires every week even when nobody plays: who's in Saturday, what time, who owes whom. The incumbent isn't 18Birdies; it's iMessage. A product that owns the gathering owns the round, the score, the bet, and the memory downstream of it. A product that owns only the scorecard is a calculator waiting to be deleted.

3 · Everyone is fighting over the round. Nobody owns the forty years.

Every competitor optimizes the two hours of play. No one is building golf's memory — the lifetime record, the rivalry that ran three decades, the last round with your father. Memory is the moat: a feed can be copied in a quarter; twelve years of Saturdays cannot be copied at all. The most defensible thing this company will ever build is the past.

PART 1Company vision

Mission & principles

Mission

Make every round of amateur golf count toward something — a cup, a rivalry, a story, a life.

Product principles

  • Real golf only. Nothing simulated, nothing about the pros. The content is your foursome, or it doesn't exist.
  • The crew is the customer. Individuals download apps; crews adopt operating systems. Every decision optimizes the group of six over the user of one.
  • Every round counts toward something. A round that lands nowhere — no cup, no rivalry, no record — is a product failure.
  • Show the work. Every number is a door. Any standing, any index, any payout opens to the rounds that produced it. Trust is a navigation feature.
  • Memory is sacred. Rounds are immutable and kept forever. We are custodians of people's Saturdays.
  • Money is tracked, never held. We are the ledger and never the bank. The needle stays friendly because the stakes stay theirs.

Design principles

  • Calm surfaces, loud moments. Airbnb whitespace as the default state; full-bleed cinema when a cup is won. Restraint makes celebration land.
  • The app knows what time it is. Tuesday night it's a clubhouse TV. Saturday 6 a.m. it's your caddie. Saturday 1 p.m. it's a scorer's tent. The interface follows golf's clock.
  • Numbers are doors. No dead-end stat anywhere in the product. Tap it, see the rounds behind it.
  • Motion imitates the ball. Every animation eases like a putt dying into the cup — fast start, long soft settle. Nothing bounces. Golf doesn't bounce; it rolls out.
  • Legible at noon. Designed for direct sunlight, gloved thumbs, and one hand holding a beverage. Outdoor contrast is an accessibility requirement, not a mode.
  • One question per screen. If a screen answers two questions, it's two screens or one too many.

Emotional goals — the week we're designing

Thursday: anticipation. The gathering card sits on Today like a ticket in a jacket pocket. Saturday: presence and consequence. Scoring takes seconds; the stakes make your ninth-hole putt mean something. Sunday: warmth. The story, the kudos, the needle from Ed. February: longing. The off-season should ache a little — "41 days to the thaw" is a feature.

The five behaviors we engineer

Post every round (counting must be automatic, entry under thirty seconds). Open on rest days (the feed and the standings give Tuesday a reason). Organize (the gathering is one tap from anywhere). Needle (kudos, chirps, rivalry pokes — sanctioned trash talk). Return every season (the calendar rhythm: raise a cup each spring, crown it each fall, relive it each winter).

PART 2Mental model

Ten objects, four of them new to the world

No screens yet. Just the nouns and how they hold each other. Everything visible in the product is one of these or a view over one.

IDENTITYGolfer

A person's permanent golf identity: name, handle, ball marker, index, home course. Owns rounds and trophies. Belongs to crews. Accrues a Record.

FACTRound

The atom. One played 9 or 18: gross, course, date, conditions, index-at-post. Immutable. Belongs to the golfer, never to a competition — competitions borrow it.

SOCIAL UNITCrew

The group chat made real: a persistent set of golfers who actually play together. Not followers, not a league roster — the foursome and its orbit. Crews outlive every competition they run. This is the unit of adoption, retention, and love.

COMPETITIONCup

Any competition a crew raises: a six-month season, a Ryder weekend, a one-day skins game, a match-play ladder, a bracket. One abstraction, many formats. Every cup has a span, a format, stakes, and a crowning. Every cup mints a trophy.

GRAVITYRivalry

Auto-detected head-to-head lifetime record between two golfers who keep ending up in the same group. "You lead Ed 34–29, and he's won the last three." Nobody creates a rivalry; the product notices it. The single best answer to "play one more round" ever designed.

SOCIALStory

What a fact looks like in public: the feed unit a round or moment becomes. Carries reactions, chirps, attestations, photos. Fans out to every crew that cares.

PLACECourse

A character, not a dropdown. Holds your personal record there, your crew's history there, the community's card data. "Papago: 41 rounds, best 84, Ed owns you here."

HONORTrophy

Minted only by cups — never given, always won. Lives permanently on the golfer's card. Engraved with the cup, the year, the crew, the margin.

LEDGERStake

What's riding: buy-ins, nassaus, skins. Tracked to the cent, held never. Settles into a who-owes-whom that the crew clears however it always has.

MEMORYThe Record

A golfer's whole life in the sport, as an artifact: every round, every cup, every rivalry, every course, composed like an autobiography rather than a database dump. The forty-year product. The moat.

How they hold each other

A Golfer joins Crews. Crews raise Cups. Cups are decided by Rounds, made fair by the index, played at Courses, with Stakes riding. Rounds become Stories; repeated pairings crystallize into Rivalries; cups mint Trophies; and every one of these accrues, permanently, to the Record. One direction of flow, no cycles, no orphans: play → count → remember.

The inversion that matters: every competitor makes the competition the container and the people its members. We make the people the container and competitions the things they do together. Leagues end; crews don't. That single inversion is why year-two retention will look like a group chat's, not an app's.

SEARCH & NOTIFICATIONS ARE OVERLAYS ON TODAY — SOCIAL PLUMBING NEVER GETS A TAB

  • Today exists because golf lives on a clock. It is not a feed; it is a stage that recomposes by state: a normal Tuesday shows stories and standings drift; the morning of a tee time it becomes your ticket — group, time, forecast, who's in; during a round it is the live card; the evening after, it leads with your story. One home that knows what time it is beats three homes that don't.
  • Crews exists because belonging precedes competition. Each crew room holds its board (the chat), its cups, its calendar, its rivalries, its stakes — the entire life of that group behind one door. There is no separate "leagues" area, because a competition without its people is a spreadsheet.
  • ⊕ Play exists because golf has three tenses and they are one gesture: Plan a gathering (before) · Play live with games (during) · Post a finished round (after). It is a verb, rendered as a sheet, never a place you get lost in.
  • You exists because the product's deepest promise is personal: the card others see, the trends only you see, and the Record that accumulates underneath both. Settings hide behind a gear; identity is never filed next to dark mode.

PART 4The user experience

What it feels like

Opening the app

Silence, then orientation. No splash video, no promotions. Today composes in 300ms: your crew's overnight stories settle in like the morning paper, and if golf is coming, the gathering card sits on top like a tee time written on a napkin. The feeling is a clubhouse porch, not a casino floor.

Starting a round

⊕ → Play now. Your usual crew is pre-staged from the gathering; the course knows you're a regular; the game picker offers Saturday's usual nassau with one tap. Twelve seconds from pocket to first tee. The phone goes back in the bag — it's a scorer, not a screen.

Finishing a round

The Walk-Off: one composed card — your score against your index, the match result, what moved in the cup, what the skins settled to. It stamps like a signed scorecard, with a haptic like a ball dropping. Then one button: post it to the crew. The work is already done; the moment is the reward.

Joining a season

A text from the crew: "we raised the cup." One link → your card slides in → the buy-in, the span, the format on a single sheet → you're on the tee sheet. Joining feels like signing an entry list, not configuring software.

Seeing friends

Ametz shot 84 at Papago and the card shows the 9th-hole birdie that saved it. You tip your cap (our kudos — a cap, not a heart). Ed chirps. The rivalry ticker under the story reminds everyone Ametz is now 2 back in the cup. Watching your friends play golf is the product's idle animation.

Competing

Standings are theater: the table breathes on Sundays, movers glow gold for a day, and every number opens to the rounds behind it, so nobody argues with the math — they argue with each other, which is the point. Pressure arrives softly: "Two counting rounds left this month."

Winning

The crowning is cinema. Full-bleed dusk screen, the trophy engraves itself letter by letter — your name, the cup, the year, the margin — then settles onto your card's case with a thock. Your crew's feed erupts on its own; you never have to say a word. The trophy is permanent. It will outlive the phone.

Losing

Dignity, then a hook. No sad trombone, no gray confetti. The final table stands as fact; your line reads second, four points shy, with the two rounds that would've flipped it one tap away. And underneath, already lit: "The rematch: spring cup opens March 1." Losing writes the first line of next season's story.

Sharing

Every story exports as a designed artifact — score, course, the one stat that tells the tale — beautiful enough for the group chat and Instagram both, watermarked like a scorecard, never like an ad. Sharing is bragging with receipts.

PART 4 · CODAFive years later

You open the Record on a February night.

Four hundred and six rounds. The graph of your index across five years, dipping the summer you got serious. Papago, 63 rounds, still your house. The rivalry page says you finally passed Ed last October — 41 to 40 — and there's a photo from that day, all of you squinting into the sun. The 2027 cup you won by a point. The 2028 one you lost by two, and it still stings, which is how you know it counted.

And there, June 2027: your dad's last round with you. An 89, windy, he birdied the ninth. You didn't know it was the last one. The Record did what records do — it kept it.

This screen is why the company exists. Everything else in this document is in service of a night like this.

PART 5High-fidelity direction — the signature screens

Eight screens treated cinematically

The full catalog follows in Part 6; these eight carry the product's soul, so they get the director's treatment.

Today — rest day

Feels like: Strava's feed × Apple Fitness Summary × a morning paper

A quiet ivory canvas, one column, 24pt gutters. At top, a whisper of state in small caps — "CUP: 2ND · 4 BACK · 11 DAYS LEFT IN JUNE" — tappable, never shouting. Then stories: white cards, 16pt radius, a soft 2dp shadow, each led by the golfer's ball marker and a single hero number (score vs index) set in 34pt tabular serif. Photos bleed edge-to-edge inside cards. Kudos is a cap-tip icon that tilts 20° with a spring when tapped.

MOTION cards settle upward 12px on load, staggered 40ms, ease-out-quint. EMPTY "Quiet week. Papago's forecast says Saturday." with a one-tap Plan chip. LOADING skeletons shaped like scorecards, shimmer at 1.2s.

Today — game day morning

Feels like: an airline boarding pass × Arc Browser's daily delight

The feed steps back; a full-width ticket takes the stage: course name in 40pt serif, tee time enormous, the four ball markers of your group in a row, live forecast, one gold button — "I'm on my way." The ticket has a faint topographic texture of the course's first hole. Below it, one line of stakes: "Nassau $5 · cup counts." The app feels like it woke up before you did.

MICRO the ticket's tee time flips to "T-minus 0:48" inside the last hour. MOTION ticket slides down from the top like it was printed.

Live Round

Feels like: Apple Watch workout × a caddie's whisper

Sunlight mode by default: near-white ground, 8pt-grid, giant tap targets. One hole at a time — hole number in 64pt, par and yardage in quiet caps, four score steppers with 56pt touch zones. The match state lives in a single pill: "2 UP · 6 TO PLAY." Wolf and skins states ride the same pill, expandable with one thumb. No map, no ads, no feed. The phone spends 4 hours in a pocket and 40 seconds in a hand.

MICRO entering a birdie draws a single gold circle around the number — the old scorecard notation, resurrected. MOTION hole transitions slide horizontally with the easing of a ball rolling out. STATE survives airplane mode; syncs on signal without ceremony.

The Walk-Off (round summary)

Feels like: Strava's post-run summary × a signed scorecard × Linear's precision

One composed artifact on ivory: score huge in serif, then three lines that each answer a question — vs your index (+2.1, gold if you beat it), the match (won 2&1, Ed pays $10), the cup (moved 4th → 2nd, with a tiny sparkline of the standings shifting). A perforated edge at the card's bottom, an engraved-style date stamp. One primary button: "Post to the crew." The share export is this exact card.

MICRO the card "stamps" on completion — 6px drop, haptic thock. HIERARCHY score → meaning → consequence, top to bottom. EMPTY never empty; it exists only when a round does.

Crew Room

Feels like: a group chat that grew up × Notion's calm structure

The crew's name hand-set in serif over a strip of member markers. Beneath: the board (chat interleaved with system moments — "SEASON CUP · ROUND 3 COUNTED"), then the room's furniture in cards: the active cup with a live two-row standings preview, the next gathering, the stakes ledger, the rivalry corner ("Jerecho–Ed: 34–29, three straight for Ed"). Everything about this group behind one door, nothing about any other group visible.

HIERARCHY talk first — the board is the room's fireplace; competition is furniture arranged around it. MICRO unread chirps pulse the crew's tab dot once, never a number badge over 9.

Cup Standings

Feels like: ESPN's tables × Apple Health Trends × sports-section typography

A dense, confident table set in tabular numerals: rank, marker, name, points, movement arrows in gold and slate. The leader's row carries a hairline gold underline — subtle enough to respect, visible enough to hunt. Tap any row: it unfolds in place to the counting rounds, each one tap from its story. A thin "month closes in 6 days" band sits under the header like a chyron. Below the table, the stakes line: pot total, split, who's paid.

MOTION when standings shift, rows glide to their new order over 600ms — no teleporting; consequence should be watchable. EMPTY pre-season: the table renders with everyone at 0 and the copy "Every cup starts level."

Rivalry

Feels like: a boxing tally card × Wikipedia's head-to-head boxes × two lockers side by side

Two markers face off at the top; between them, the lifetime tally in enormous serif: 34–29. Underneath, the eras: streaks, biggest rout, the courses each man owns, the year the lead last changed hands. A shared timeline of every round played together runs down the page like a spine. One button, gold: "Settle it Saturday" — which opens Plan with both of you staged.

MICRO when the tally changes, the number flips like an old scoreboard odometer. EMOTION this page should make one of the two men slightly, productively angry.

The Record

Feels like: Apple's Year in Review × a leather club ledger × a museum wall

The one screen set fully in the editorial serif voice. Chapters, not filters: The Numbers (lifetime rounds, best year, index arc drawn as one continuous line), The Places (courses as engraved cards with your history at each), The People (rivalries and crews through time), The Honors (the trophy wall, engraved), The Rounds (the complete archive, every single one, searchable by feel — "windy days," "with Dad," "at Papago in October"). Scrolling it should feel like turning pages.

MOTION almost none — memory doesn't animate; it settles. Chapter transitions are slow cross-fades, 400ms. LOADING chapters resolve oldest-last, like a photo developing.

States & motion, globally

Empty states are invitations written in golf voice — never "No data": "No rounds yet. The card fills as you play." Loading is skeletal, never spinning — scorecard-shaped placeholders, one shimmer, 1.2s period. Microinteraction glossary: cap-tip (kudos), gold circle (birdie), stamp (round posted), odometer flip (rivalry), letter-engrave (trophy), row-glide (standings). Six signatures, reused everywhere, learned once.

PART 7Design system

“Morning tee sheet, trophy room at night”

Color philosophy

Two worlds, both real places in golf. Daylight (default): warm paper whites and pine-green ink — the morning tee sheet, built for sunlight and calm. Dusk (dark mode): deep green-black with champagne gold — the trophy room, where honors and finales live. Gold is spent only on what is earned: leads, wins, trophies, movement upward. It is never decoration. One dawn blue exists for links and live states. Semantic red appears only for money owed and deletion.

Typography

Three voices. A modern text serif (the voice of memory and honor): headlines, the Record, trophies, hero numbers on story cards. A confident grotesque (the voice of now): UI, tables, live play — always with tabular numerals; scores must stack like scores. A quiet mono (the voice of the scorer's tent): eyebrows, timestamps, stakes, system moments. Type scale on a 1.25 ratio from 12 to 64; hero numbers may break the scale because a 64pt score is the point.

Space, shape, elevation

8pt grid; screen gutters 20–24pt; cards breathe with 16–20pt internal padding and Airbnb-grade section gaps (32–48pt). Corner radius: 16pt cards, 10pt controls, 24pt sheets — friendly but not bubbly. Elevation is Linear-flat: hairline borders carry structure, shadows exist at exactly two levels (resting 2dp whisper, lifted 8dp for sheets), and nothing floats without a reason.

Iconography & illustration

Icons: 2px rounded strokes, drawn on the same grid, golf-native where it matters (the cap-tip, the pennant, the marker). Illustration style: engraved-scorecard linework — single-weight etchings like an old club ledger, used for empty states and ceremony, never cartoons. Ball markers are the avatar system: 24+ archetype shapes, collectible, permanent.

Photography

Only the members' own. Golden-hour, phone-shot, honest — sunburned, wind-blown, gloriously amateur. The product's photography direction is a constraint, not a style guide: we never show golf the members didn't play. No stock swings. No pros.

Motion

One physics: the roll-out — fast start, long soft settle (cubic ease-out, 250–400ms), the way a putt dies at the hole. Six signature microinteractions (cap-tip, gold circle, stamp, odometer, engrave, row-glide) and no others without a design review. Celebrations are earned and rare; the crowning is the only full-screen takeover in the product. Every animation respects reduced-motion by settling instantly.

Accessibility

Contrast AAA for scores and money, AA minimum everywhere. Sunlight mode: auto-engaging high-contrast live-play palette driven by ambient light — designed for noon glare and polarized sunglasses. Touch targets 44pt minimum, 56pt during live play (gloves). Color never carries meaning alone: movement arrows pair with direction, gold pairs with rank. Full Dynamic Type support including the tables, which reflow rather than truncate. Every number-as-door is a labeled button for screen readers: "Second place, 41 points, opens counting rounds."

PART 9Roadmap

From one crew to the sport's memory

Version 1 — the wedge (one crew, one cup, whole)

One crew runs one season perfectly: gather → play → post → count → crown. The feed, the card, the index, stakes tracked, the Walk-Off, the crowning. Nothing else — no courses directory, no rivalry pages, no Record. V1's only metric: does the crew raise a second cup without being asked?

Year 2 — gravity

Rivalries ship and change everything (retention becomes personal). The Record v1. Courses become characters. Multi-crew life. Season films — a two-minute cinematic "Your Year in Golf" every December that gets shared beyond the app and becomes the growth engine. Settle-up deep links. The off-season countdown.

Year 5 — the institution

Clubs and munis run their leagues, member-guests, and club championships on CupSeason — the B2B layer arrives because two real crews asked to play each other, not before. The CupSeason index is accepted wherever amateurs play for fun. City ladders connect crews into an amateur season. The memory vault: scan the shoebox of old scorecards; the Record reaches back before the app existed.

Year 10 — the operating system, for real

The default way amateur golf is organized in every country that plays it. A golfer's Record spans her junior years to her senior club. And one weekend each fall: the CupSeason Open — the largest golf tournament ever played, tens of thousands of crews on ten thousand courses, one distributed leaderboard, handicapped to a single table. Amateur golf gets what the pros always had: a history that keeps score.

FINAL RULEThe test every pixel must pass

"Does this make someone want to play one more round with their friends?"

Applied honestly, it already killed features during this exercise: AI swing analysis (improves a swing, summons no foursome — cut). GPS rangefinders (a utility war we don't want to win — partner, cut). Pro golf news and content (about someone else's golf — cut). Public follower counts (turns crews into audiences — cut). Streaks and daily-active mechanics (guilt is not anticipation — cut).

What survives is everything in this document: the gathering that gets you there, the cup that makes it count, the rivalry that makes it personal, and the Record that makes it permanent. Play one more round. We'll keep it forever.
